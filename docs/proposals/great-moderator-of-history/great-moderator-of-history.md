# The Great Moderator of History Proposal

## Introduction

The history of the world seem to have a sort of a mechanism that moderates the course of history. This mechanism is what I call the "Great Moderator of History". A sort of their equivalent of so-called 'market correction', if you will. The Great Moderator of History is a force that acts to correct the course of history when it goes too far in one direction or another. It oscillates between the extremes of utopia and dystopia.

### Utopia and Dystopia

#### Utopia
  1. Over-optimism in the 1920s: The people thought that poverty was going to be eradicated, but it was not. Counter-example: The Great Depression in the 1930s.
  2. The End of History in the 1990s: People thought that liberal democracy was the end of history, but it was not. Counter-example: The rise of authoritarianism in the 21st century.
  3. The Enlightenment in the 18th century: People thought that reason and science were going to solve all problems, but they did not. Counter-example: The Reign of Terror during the French Revolution.
  4. The Space Race in the 1960s: People thought we were going to colonize space by the end of 20th century, but we did not. Counter-example: The continued exploration and study of space without colonization.
  5. The rise of the internet in the 1990s: People thought that the internet was going to solve all problems, but it did not. Counter-example: The rise of social media and its negative effects on society.
  6. The war to end all wars in the early 20th century: People thought that World War I was going to be the last war, but it was not. Counter-example: The outbreak of World War II in 1939.
  7. The end of the Cold War in the early 1990s: People thought that the end of the Cold War was going to lead to a new era of peace and prosperity, but it did not. Counter-example: The rise of new conflicts and tensions in the post-Cold War world.
  8. The 'unsinkable' nature of the Titanic in 1912: People thought that the Titanic was unsinkable, but it was not. Counter-example: The sinking of the Titanic after hitting an iceberg. An example of hubris leading to a downfall, which is a common theme in history.
  9. The Green Revolution in the mid-20th century: People thought that the Green Revolution was going to solve world hunger, but it did not. Counter-example: The continued existence of hunger and food insecurity in many parts of the world.
  10. The rise of the automobile in the early 20th century: People thought that the automobile was going to solve all transportation problems, but it did not. Counter-example: The rise of traffic congestion, pollution, and the continued reliance on fossil fuels for transportation.
  11. The rise of communism in the early 20th century: People thought that communism was going to create a utopian society, but it did not. Counter-example: The rise of authoritarian regimes and economic failures in communist countries.

#### Dystopia
  1. The Black Death in the 14th century: People truly thought the world was ending, but it did not. Counter-example: The Renaissance in the 15th and 16th centuries.
  2. The fall of the Roman Empire in the 5th century: People thought that civilization was going to collapse, but it did not. Counter-example: The Byzantine Empire and the Holy Roman Empire.
  3. The Cuban Missile Crisis in 1962: People thought that nuclear war was imminent, but it did not happen. Counter-example: The detente between the US and the Soviet Union in the 1970s.
  4. The Y2K bug in 2000: People thought that the world was going to end, but it did not. Counter-example: The continued functioning of global computer systems.
  5. The COVID-19 pandemic in 2020: People thought that the world was going to end, but it did not. Counter-example: The rapid development and distribution of vaccines.
  6. The Russian invasion of Ukraine in 2022: People thought that World War III was going to happen, but it did not. Counter-example: The international diplomatic efforts to prevent escalation.
  7. People thought (as seem to be apparent in the 1970s, indicated by the West German policy of Ostpolitik) that the Cold War division of Germany was permanent, but it was not. Counter-example: The reunification of Germany in 1990.
  8. People thought that the Troubles in Northern Ireland was either never going to end or that it was going to end in a violent reunification of Ireland, but it did not. This was evident in an episode of *Star Trek: The Next Generation*, titled "The High Ground", where Data mentioned the Irish reunification of 2024. Counter-example: The Good Friday Agreement in 1998 and the subsequent peace process.
  9. People thought that Napoleon was unbeatable, but he was not. Counter-example: The Battle of Waterloo in 1815 and the Leipzig Campaign in 1813.
  10. People thought that the Germans was unbeatable in World War II, but they were not. Counter-example: The D-Day invasion in 1944 and the Battle of Stalingrad in 1942-1943.

#### Ambiguous (not necessarily utopia or dystopia)
  1. People thought that the Soviet Union would continue to exist by the 23rd century or later. This is evident in *Star Trek IV: The Voyage Home* (1986), where the Soviet Union is still in existence in the 23rd century, as indicated by the mention of Leningrad in the dialogue. This is also evident in the surprise with which the West were taken by the collapse of the Soviet Union in 1991. Counter-example: The dissolution of the Soviet Union in 1991.
  2. People thought that the French Revolution was going to lead to a utopian society, but it did not. This is evident in the initial optimism and support for the revolution, as well as the subsequent Reign of Terror and the rise of Napoleon. Counter-example: The eventual establishment of a more stable government and the spread of revolutionary ideals in other countries.

#### Unclear (need more data)
  1. Did the "Space Seed" episode of *Star Trek: The Original Series* (1967) genuinely reflect the fears of genetic engineering and eugenics in the 1960s, or was it simply a science fiction story? Counter-example: The continued ethical debates and regulations surrounding genetic engineering and eugenics in the present day.
